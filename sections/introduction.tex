\section{Introduction}
    The field of High Performance Computing (HPC) has been undergoing a shift towards extreme heterogeneity in computational architectures. For decades, the integration of General Purpose Graphic Processing Units (GPGPUs) along with traditional Central Computing Units (CPUs) has defined the limits of its computational power. However, in recent years, the demand to stimulate complex molecular dynamics along with combinatorial optimization has revealed the limitation of classical architectures. Issues such as the curse of dimensionality and the exponential scaling of Hilbert space have redirected focus towards the potential of Quantum Processing Units (QPUs). QPUs have emerged, not as a replacement for classical clusters, but instead as specialized accelerators that are capable of handing specific computational kernels that are otherwise intractable for classical bits ~\cite{rallis2025interfacingquantumcomputingsystems}. Hybrid frameworks are the goal to work towards to achieve new ground in the fields of material science, complex optimization, and cryptographic analysis.  


    Currently, in the era of Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum (NISQ), the most viable path towards achieving the "quantum advantage" is through the development of hybrid quantum-classical algorithms ~\cite{rallis2025interfacingquantumcomputingsystems}. Algorithms such as the Variational Quantum Eigensolver (VQE) utilizes a feedback loop in which a classical optimizer iteratively refines the parameters to be entered in a quantum circuit. By offloading the computationally heavy classical optimization loop to the heterogeneous classical resources, QPUs solely focus on the execution of quantum circuits and measuring the resulting expectation values. This bypasses the limits of coherency from QPUs, as presently the issue occurs in executing fault-tolerant algorithms, such as the Quantum Phase Estimation (QPE) algorithm, which requires gate depths and long run-times that exceed the temporal qubit coherence, yielding decolorant or incorrect results. If long-term coherency in qubits was achieved, the QPE algorithm would be able to provide an exponential speedup compared to classical methods in determining eigenvalues in large problems ~\cite{peruzzo2014variational}. Classical HPC structures, using MPI, CUDA and multi-node CPU/GPU excel with heavy parallel numerical workloads and large scale simulations, yet classical systems will struggle with simulations where the dimension of the problem grows exponentially alongside the size of the physical system being observed. This challenge within electronic structure calculations is addressed through hybrid systems that allow the quantum component to map the system's wavefunction directly onto the qubits, while classical components refine the variational parameters to be inputted into quantum circuits \cite{rallis2025interfacingquantumcomputingsystems}.
    

    Despite the theoretical promise of this hybrid collaboration, a significant architectural gap remains: currently, there is a lack of specialized middleware to bridge the quantum and classical components. Classical HPC frameworks that are optimized for MPI and CUDA are not developed to communicate directly with quantum control stacks, instead requiring researchers to manually handle the complex data serialization and the network latencies. Existing quantum programming frameworks, such as Qiskit and Cirq, excel at circuit-level QPU control but lack the deep integration with distributed HPC environments that would be required for seamless communication. Without a standardized middleware to connect the environments, researchers face issues with high tail latency when synchronizing both HPC nodes and cloud-based QPUs, leading to performances bottlenecks that eliminates the possibility of a potential speedup offered by a hybrid system. This, coupled with the complexity of data serialization and the heterogeneous execution environments between quantum and classical systems leaves a barrier for researchers to overcome in large-scale quantum-classical integration. 

    The proposed solution in this project is the development of a hybrid quantum-classical software stack that builds the middleware layer needed to abstract the hardware and automates the dispatching of tasks. This software stack will design a clear, intuitive API to abstract the backend of the cloud-based QPUs to allow researchers or developers to bypass these communication complexities. The stack will include automated dispatching logic, the development of HPC connectors for the Qiskit framework, and implemented classical kernels using MPI/CUDA to parallelize the classical optimization subroutines. Benchmarking will occur through a performance analysis of VQE workflows conducted on multi-node clusters. This project is to demonstrate measurable progress toward "quantum advantage," with a 2-3x speedup in the end-to-end wall-clock time when being compared to the single-node and non-optimized classical implementations. Ultimately, the purpose of this work is to design, implement, and benchmark a robust, open-source HPC software framework that achieves a measurable workflow acceleration through integrating QPUs with multi-node classical resources.
