

\section{Introduction}
    The field of High Performance Computing (HPC) has been undergoing a shift towards extreme heterogeneity in computational architectures. For decades, the integration of General Purpose Graphic Processing Units (GPGPUs), along with traditional Central Computing Units (CPUs) has defined the limits of this field's computational power. However in recent years, the demand to stimulate complex molecular dynamics, along with combinatorial optimization, has revealed the current limitations of classical architectures, where issues such as the curse of dimensionality and the exponential scaling of Hilbert space have directed focus towards the potential of Quantum Processing Units (QPUs). QPUs have emerged, not to be a replacement for classical clusters, but instead to be utilized as specialized accelerators capable of handing specific computational kernels that are otherwise intractable for classical bits ~\cite{rallis2025interfacingquantumcomputingsystems}. Hybrid frameworks are the current goal to work towards in order to achieve new ground in the fields of material science, complex optimization, and cryptographic analysis.  

    Currently, in the era of Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum (NISQ), the most viable path towards achieving the "quantum advantage" will be through the development of hybrid quantum-classical algorithms ~\cite{rallis2025interfacingquantumcomputingsystems}. Algorithms such as the Variational Quantum Eigensolver (VQE) utilizes a feedback loop in which a classical optimizer iteratively refines the parameters that will be entered into the quantum circuit. The strategy of offloading the computationally heavy classical optimization loop to the heterogeneous classical resources, allows QPUs solely focus on the execution of quantum circuits and measuring the resulting expectation values. This assists in bypasing the modern day limitations of coherency in QPUs, as presently the issue occurs during the execution of fault-tolerant algorithms. An example of this would be the Quantum Phase Estimation (QPE) algorithm, which requires gate depths and long runtimes that exceed the temporal qubit coherence, resulting in decolorant or incorrect results. If long-term coherency in qubits was achieved, the QPE algorithm would be able to provide an exponential speedup compared to classical methods when tasked with determining eigenvalues in large, complex problems ~\cite{peruzzo2014variational}. Classical HPC structures, using MPI, CUDA and multi-node CPU/GPU clusters, excel with heavy parallel numerical workloads and large scale simulations. Yet, classical systems will struggle with simulations where the dimension of the problem grows exponentially alongside the size of the physical system being observed. This challenge occurs specifically within electronic structure calculations and can be addressed through the usage of hybrid systems that allow the quantum component to map the system's wavefunction directly onto the qubits, while classical components refine the variational parameters to be inputted into quantum circuits \cite{rallis2025interfacingquantumcomputingsystems}.
    
    Despite the theoretical potential of hybrid-computational collaboration, presently a significant architectural gap remains: there is a lack of specialized middleware to bridge the quantum and classical components. Classical HPC frameworks (ones that are optimized for MPI and CUDA) are not developed to communicate directly with quantum control stacks. This requires researchers to manually handle the complex data serialization and the network latencies that occur during this communication. Existing quantum programming frameworks, such as Qiskit and Cirq, excel at circuit-level QPU control but utimately lack the deep integration with distributed HPC environments that would be required for seamless communication between the systems. Without a standardized middleware, quantum researchers face issues with high tail latency when synchronizing both HPC nodes and cloud-based QPUs, leading to performances bottlenecks that eliminates the possibility of a potential speedup offered by a hybrid system. This, coupled with the complexity of data serialization and a potential lack of understand of the heterogeneous execution environments between quantum and classical systems leaves a barrier for researchers to overcome in larger scale quantum-classical integration projects. 

    The proposed solution of this project is the development of a hybrid quantum-classical software stack that builds the middleware layer needed to abstracts the hardwares and automates the dispatching of tasks. This software stack will design a clear, intuitive API to abstract the backend of the cloud-based QPU to allow quantum researchers or developers to bypass these communication complexities. This hybrid stack will include automated dispatching logic, the development of HPC connectors for the Qiskit framework, and implemented classical kernels using MPI/CUDA to parallelize the classical optimization subroutines. Benchmarking will occur through a performance analysis of VQE workflows, conducted on multi-node GPU clusters. This project aims to demonstrate measurable progress towards the "quantum advantage," with a 2-3x speedup in the end-to-end wall-clock time when being compared to the single-node and non-optimized classical implementations. Ultimately, the purpose of this work is the design, implementation, and benchmarking a robust, open-source HPC software framework that achieves a measurable workflow acceleration through integrating QPUs with multi-node classical resources.
