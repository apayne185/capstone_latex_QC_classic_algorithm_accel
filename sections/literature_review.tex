

\section{Literature Review}
This section explores the evolution of hybrid quantum-classical computing across three main domains: quantum hardware limitations in the NISQ era, the emergence of variational algorithms, and the architecture requirements for HPC orchestration within this proposed stack. Within this software stack, the terms 'middleware' and 'orchestration' are referring to the software layers that manage the handshake (the communication) between the remote, cloud-based QPU backend and the distributed classical resources. Hybrid acceleration is defined, as not just the integration of quantum circuit execution, but also by the maximized throughput by parallelizing the classical optimization loop and reducing communication latency by batching. The following sections within this literature review aim to evaluate how these existing frameworks support the design choices made in this stack while also identifying the performance gaps this project aims to bridge. 

\subsection{Fundamentals of Quantum Hardware} 
Quantum computing has evolved considerably in recent decades, as the promises of this technology to significantly boost computational power and enhance problem solving capabilities of existing systems are objectives that have become closer to reality every year \cite{rallis2025interfacingquantumcomputingsystems}. The potential speedups offered could be polynomial and super-polynomial over sets of classical algorithms \cite{cai2023quantumerrormitigation}. Due to their unique capabilities, future quantum computers are expected to handle computationally intensive problems, including a set of NP-hard and NP-complete problems that have proven intractible on classical computers. The time expected to solve these problems grows exponentially with the size of the problem, requiring time and resources that is difficult in classical-only systems \cite{auyeung2023nphard}. However, during our current era of Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum (NISQ), quantum hardware is limited and unable to be used alone for large-scale problems. Therefore, the most common and effective method of utilizing quantum computers is by having it interfaced and used alongside a classical computing system, typically in a HPC environment \cite{rallis2025interfacingquantumcomputingsystems}, otherwise known as the development of a hybrid system. 

Quantum hardware employs qubits (as opposed to a classical bit), which exploit the unique properties of quantum mechanics, including entanglement, superposition, and quantum measurement. Quantum entanglement is a phenomena in which two qubits become interdependent ('entangled'), linking them over any measurable distance. Under this, these qubits states cannot be described independently, with operations applied to one instantly applied to the other. Superposition is one of the most powerful principles in quantum mechanics, allowing a quantum system (qubit) to exist in a multitude of states simultaneously. Quantum measurement (which is extremely different when compared to classical measurement), collapses a qubit into a single fundamental state based on its quantum wave function (0 or 1). However, the benefits introduced through the usage of qubits is counterbalanced by their major physical limitations, such as decoherence, scalability, and quantum error correction. Qubits are highly sensitive to their physical environment, with minor disturbances causing loss of quantum information and unstable quantum states (known as decoherence). These quantum disturbances are labeled as "noise", which refers to any environmental disturbances or hardware imperfections \cite{cai2023quantumerrormitigation}. Due to this, issues with scalability arise as only a limited amount of qubits can be managed at a time while maintaining their reliability and coherence. Quantum Error Correction (QEC) is another major challenge, as the fragility of qubits leads to errors when operating that are complex to correct, making reliability difficult to guarantee in quantum results. \cite{oukaira2025quantum}. 

Quantum computers are currently within the earlier stages of development, in which several alternative quantum computer hardware options are being explored. This includes superconducting qubits, trapped-ion qubits, photonic qubits, neutral atoms, and spin qubits in silicon. Each version has its own advantages and disadvantages to its performance that are being continually researched and developed by technology companies and quantum research groups around the world. The challenges of error correction, qubit coherence, and scalability are still being overcome in order to develop a quantum computer that is practical, large-scale, and fault-tolerant\cite{rallis2025interfacingquantumcomputingsystems}. Presently, Quantum Error Mitigation (QEM) addresses this problem and it is acknowledged that progress is still ongoing in quantum hardware improvement, with the field instead focusing on developing immediate improvements to quantum information processing with existing hardware\cite{cai2023quantumerrormitigation}. By applying a short-depth quantum circuits to a simple initial state and then estimating a recent observables expectation values for a future output of the algorithm\cite{peruzzo2014variational}, issues such as short coherency and noise can be bypassed. QEM is an algorithmic scheme that aims to minimize noise-induced bias in the expectation values returned on noisy hardware by post-processing the outputs from an ensemble of circuit runs (using circuits at the same noise level as the original circuit or above). This reduces the noise for the collective whole of the ensemble, but not on a individual level \cite{cai2023quantumerrormitigation}. 

\subsection{The Emergence of Variational Quantum Algorithms}
The foundational framework within this research is the Variational Quantum Eigensolver (VQE), which was first introduced by Peruzzo et al in 2014.~\cite{peruzzo2014variational}. Within this paper, the researchers used a photonic quantum processor to demonstrate its ability in calculating the ground state-energy of a molecule (specifically the $He-H^{+}$ molecule (2 electrons, 6 Pauli terms, requiring 2 qubits)) through a combination of a quantum circuit and a classical optimization loop. This paper's methodology and results was critical to this stack's development as it redefined the role of the QPU from a standalone component to a coprocessor within a classical framework. However, it should be noted that the molecule chosen to simulate in Perruzzo's research was a minimal possible VQE experiment, chosen to be a proof-of-concept on the hardware that had existed in 2014. $He-H^{+}$ molecule only has 2 electrons (a small Hamiltonian), allowing for the lowest complexity required for a VQE circuit, along with only 6 Pauli terms, several of which are diagonal and require no entangling gates to measure. This molecular selection for work is intentional as their goal was to effectiveness of the VQE algorithm on the avaliable 2-mode photonic quantum chip. This proposed stack will extend the basis of their research on VQE's to handle larger, more complex molecules. VQEs are one of two major Variational Quantum Algorithms (VQAs) (the second being the Quantum Approximate Optimization Algorithm (QAOA)), used in solving optimization problems that exist across every industry and can be difficult to solve due to incomplete or uncertain data, difficulties in examining the problem at hand, or simply due to being an NP-hard problem. These algorithms require an ansatz, a parameterized quantum circuit whose form dictates what variational parameters are used and how to train them to minimize a cost function. Ansatzs are an initial guess at the functional form of a wavelength expressed as a tuneable circuit. This process is adaptive, requiring the use of hybrid resources to allow a quantum circuit to navigate this parameterized cost function (which is where a hypersurface is encoded) in order to estimate a global minima that corresponds to the solution of the problem at hand ~\cite{auyeung2023nphard}.  MaCaskey et al. \cite{mccaskey2019quantumchemistrybenchmarknearterm} discusses the current options for ansatzes: the Unitary Coupled Cluster (UCC) and the Hardware Effective (HWE) ansatz.  UCC provides a method for electron number-conserving while describing trial wavefunctions but yields a circuit with a large depth even for smaller systems, while HWE offers a shorter circuit depth and supports a larger parameter space, but it prepares states with a varying number of electrons that yield unphysical results. For chemistry-orientated approaches, UCC would be the logical choice as it is designed to mimic the movements and interactions of electrons within molecules, taking in the considerations of physicals systems. UCC describes the wavefunction as $$|\psi_{UCC}\rangle = e^{\hat{T}_1 + \hat{T}_2 + \hat{T}_3 + \hat{T}_4 + \ldots + \hat{T}_N - h.c.}|\phi_0\rangle$$, with the sum representing every possible way eelctrons can be rearranged across orbitals. However, the depth required by the circuit created by this ansatz is deep, which can be impractical on the current NISQ hardware's coherency limitations. A truncuated version of UCC, UCCSD, is cut at doubles, describing the wavefunction as  $$|\psi_{UCCSD}\rangle = e^{\hat{T}_1 + \hat{T}_2 - h.c.}|\phi_0\rangle$$. As explained by Romero et al., stopping at doubles still captures the vast majority of smaller molecules electron correlation energy, with triples and beyond contributing diminishing returns compared to their circuit costs \cite{romero2018strategiesquantumcomputingmolecular}.  HWE is a hardware-considerate approach, with shallower circuits that can be executed with fewer complications on noisy hardware and utilizes gates that are specific for the hardware optimal performance. However, research has proven convergence to be more difficult for HWE as it struggles with barren plateaus due to its random starting parameters (random, abstract mathematical rotations instead of electron movements). 

Perruzo et al. established through the usage of the Variational Principle, stating that the ground energy state $E_0$ will always be lesser than or equal to the expectation value of $H$ (the time-independent Hamiltonian that is calculated with the trial Hamiltonian wavefunction $|\psi(\theta)\rangle$). 
\begin{equation}
     E_0 \leq \langle \psi(\theta) | \hat{H} | \psi(\theta) \rangle
    \label{eq:variational_principle}
\end{equation}
 By varying $\psi$ until the expectation value is minimized, the VQE can use this approximation to find the ground state wavefunction. ~\cite{fitzpatrickVariational}. By utilizing this principle, we can reduce the coherence time requirements of the NISQ era, where currently the field has quantum computers with only 50-100 qubits that are very susceptible to noise, leading to high error rates within their results. This, coupled with a lack of full error correction, limits quantum circuit depth and means fault-tolerant algorithms are currently unable to run on modern NISQ quantum computers. The term NISQ was coined by John Preskill in 2018, where he established in our current years we have imperfect control over intermediate, constantly evolving quantum computers that will assist largely in the areas of chemistry and optimization \cite{preskill2018quantum}. This affirms that in present day, hybrid systems that mitigate the computationally heavy work of a system to the classical components will be crucial to successful usage of QPUs for advancements in these fields. 

Within the experiments run by Perruzo et al., to compensate for the noise and temporally limited coherency of qubits they offloaded the objective function evaluation to the QPU with a classical Nelder-Mead simplex optimizer (chosen due to its ability to navigate noisy environments) to refine the parameters to be re-entered into the quantum circuit. Their VQE method was a realistic alternative to the Quantum Phase Estimation (QPE) method, where the time a quantum computer would need to remain coherent (determined by the number $O(p^{-1})$) of successive applications of the time-evolution operator $e^{-iHt}$ - possibly requiring millions to billions of quantum gates) exceeds the current limits of coherency in quantum hardware. However, if advancements in hardware were made, QPE would offer an exponential speedup over classical methods. VQE is introduced as an alternative due to how it replaces the long coherent evolution required by QPE by preparing the quantum state and exploiting Quantum Expectation Estimation (QEE) as a subroutine. This processing begins with feeding parameters defined in the CPU into $N$ amount of quantum modules in the QPU, which compute the Hamiltonian ($\langle H_i \rangle$) for each module. These individual Hamiltonian's ($\langle H_i \rangle$) will then be passed back into a classical adder in the CPU to compute ($\langle H \rangle$). A classical minimization algorithm, in Perruzo et al. paper's case was Nelder-Mead (NM), which determined new state parameters to be fed back to the QPU. Nelder-Mead is a gradient free optimizer that aims to find a local minimum of an objective function within a multi-dimensional space. This method would require several, short-term iterations within a QPU where the computationally heavy work is completed on classical computers while quantum components handle the preparation and measurement of quantum states (which is a classically impossible task), bypassing the limitations of long term coherency in quantum computers and therefore providing more accurate results. However, in a comparison study done by Pellow-Jarman et al. \cite{pellow2021classicoptimizers} between sets of gradient-based and gradient-free classical optimizers for a Variational Quantum Linear Solver, another gradient-free optimizer, Simultaneous Perturbation Stochastic Approximation (SPSA) outperformed all other contestants in state vector simulation.  With the introduction of realistic noise models, SPSA performed the best while NM and others performed better under noise-free conditions, making them a less desireable choice when interacting with physical qubits. Their comparisons were conducted on quantum simulators, which model similar experiences (noise) as within an actual quantum computer. Within the current NISQ era, the most appropriate approach is to use a optimizer that is robust in a noisy environment, such as SPSA. 

While the results of Perruzo et al. paper proved the viability of the hybrid approach, it was conducted on a small-scale, integrated system where classical quantum communication overhead is negligible. Their setup consisted of a photonic chip that was directly interfaced with a local classical controller, resulting in low latency in the time to send new variational parameters to the QPU and receive measured expectation values in the parameter-update loop. This physically tight setup is a unrealistic model for modern research experiment environments where HPC clusters and QPUs would be physically distant. Through their reliance on a local handshake, Perruzo et al. avoided the serialization overhead and network latencies that define modern cloud based quantum computing setups. 

In the event of VQE scaling to larger molecules (beyond their $He-H^{+}$ model) and higher-dimensional Hilbert spaces, the number of required circuit evaluations and classical optimization steps would grow exponentially. The results determined in this paper demonstrate the VQE as robust against certain hardware errors, such as the Poissonian noise introduced through their photon source and physical imperfections in the beam splitters and waveguides. However, Perruzo et al. did not address the throughput bottleneck, where in a cloud based implementation (lacking on-site quantum hardware) every handshake between the classical optimizer and the QPU would result in a Round-Trip Time (RTT) penalty. If the hybrid software stack is not optimized, the potential time spend waiting for the data transmission could exceed the time spent on actual computation, leading to a "Quantum-Classical Communication Wall", as discussed by Weder et al. \cite{weder2021quantum}, who demonstrated within cloud native environments an observable latency is introduced through the remote hardware access and optimized task placement. They argue that without a middleware layer that is capable of efficient resource dispatching, the "communication-to-computation" ratio will scale poorly, which can negate the potential speedup offered within hybrid quantum-classical systems. This stack can address this architectural inefficiencies identified by Weder et al. through a stack that prioritizes low-latency dispatching and parallel classical preprocessing. 


\subsection{Classical-Quantum Middleware and Orchestration}
High Performance Computing is a version of classical computing systems that extends the ability of a traditional system beyond the conventional, to using clusters of processing units (this can extend up to several thousands of processing units, including CPUs and GPUs, in a single HPC unit). While HPC relies on the same digital logic circuits and Boolean algebra as conventional classical computers, they now include specialized accelerators such as GPUs, FPGAs, TPUs, and other units that allow HPC systems to handle diverse tasks and parallel processing workloads. This form of computing has become more significant over time, now adopted by a wide range of fields who require the advanced computational power -- such as Artificial Intelligence research or the Automotive Industry, Energy prediction, and many more \cite{rallis2025interfacingquantumcomputingsystems}. 

The architectural foundation for a hardware independent quantum integration process was developed by McCaskey et al. \cite{mccaskey2018xacc} within their eXtreme ACCelerator (XACC) framework. They acknowledge quantum programmings current issue of a variety of companies, including Rigetti, IBM, Google, etc., all providing different quantum programming languages that targets each version of quantum hardware. However, none of these companies have provided an efficient method of integration between the software and the hardware, especially not one that transcends the differences in computational paradigms or hardware specifications between companies quantum computers. This situation leaves users, often researchers, to develop the often complex communication between the quantum-classical components, including the compiler workflow steps of instruction scheduling, routing, and error mitigation. Without a programming background, users would face difficulties navigating the frameworks and can expect significant technical roadblocks. XACC consists of a system-level software infrastructure, where their low-level software manages the QPU hardware, treating it as a specialized coprocessor which allows researchers to offload quantum kernels from the classical host to various backend accelerators using a unified Application Programming Interface (API). By abstracting the hardware and focusing on the development of a intuitive Pythonic interface that interacts with the QPU, they have successfully set the standard for quantum interoperability 

The architecture of XACC is defined by three layers that decouple the high-level algorithm from the low-level architecture: 

        \begin{enumerate}
            \item Compilers (Frontend): Translates the high-level quantum kernels (the code written by developers), often written in languages such as XASM or Quil, into a hardware-agnostic intermediate representation (IR). This is the creation of an instance of IR for the specific quantum programming language.

            \item Intermediate Representation (IR): Provides a lower-level abstract representation of the quantum kernel for usability across a variety of accelerators (QPUs) and programming languages. This is a polymorphic object model, represented by an Abstract Syntax Tree (AST) that allows their middleware to perform "visitor" patterns (scripts can move through circuit and either optimize or compile it without knowing the original language\slash frontend).

            \item Accelerators (Backend): Layer that provides the execution logic for communication with the backend, which can be physical QPUs or virtual simulations. This computation is separate from the data (defined as the AcceleratorBuffer), which registers qubits (results of measurements and their metadata - error gates, execution time, etc.). This abstraction is relevant to managing the handshake results and storing them locally.
        \end{enumerate}

While the XACC framework did successfully achieve their goal of language and hardware portability, its implementations focused mainly on compiler level integration with a single-host execution model. They concentrated on how a classical computer should communicate with a quantum computer - architectural connection - without addressing the bottlenecks encountered with high-scalability in classical optimization subroutines which require distributed, multi-node resources. The XACC model is synchronous, where the classical host pushes a circuit to the QPU then waits for a result. This functionality works within a single-host model, but will limit the system if it grows to multi-host. 

This proposed stack builds upon XACC and its modular philosophy but now emphasizes orchestration priority. XACC manages an language/hardware agnostic approach to manage the handshake between a single CPU and a QPU, which will be developed and scaled further in this thesis with the software stack implementing a massively parallel dispatcher. By integrating Message Passing Interface (MPI) and CUDA, this project will extend the concept of the quantum accelerator model into the domain of high performance cluster computing. The classical bottlenecks of the VQE algorithm loop will be distributed with CUDA across a heterogeneous CPU-GPU cluster to effectively handle the performance limitations that occur in a single-host middleware layer. This software stack will also handle asynchronous execution, as otherwise in a multi-node HPC environment the entire cluster would remain idle as a single cloud-based QPU processes a circuit. Through the implementation of non-blocking calls, this middleware will extend XACC's logic in order to allow HPC nodes to continue onto classical preprocessing or error mitigation tasks during the QPU's handshake. 

The XACC architectural model was also developed with local simulation and direct access to a QPU, avoiding the reality of our modern "cloud-native" systems where a QPU is not locally available and therefore experiences a high latency handshake between the systems components. The stack developed in this thesis accounts for the "Communication Wall" \cite{weder2021quantum}, where the dispatcher manages a workflow with classical CUDA-accelerated nodes and a remote, latent QPU. 


\subsection{HPC Integration and Parallelization} 
Early quantum-classical hybrid systems research mostly focused on the feasibility of quantum kernels within isolated and experimental setups, but Bayraktar et al.\cite{bayraktar2023cuquantum} guided the academic focus instead towards the computational intensity of the classical overhead, establishing the need for hardware-accelerated hybrid computation. Within their research, they identified the source of this bottleneck to be the classical simulation and expectation value estimation, scaling exponentially ($2^n$ ) with the number of qubits ($n$) and the corresponding complexity of the variational ansatz increases. This is a bottleneck that a serial CPU architecture cannot manage. While the QPU manages the state preparation and measurement, the classical host is assigned to handle the high-dimensional numerical optimization along with the processing of the expectation values. Bayraktar et al. evaluates the NVIDIA cuQuantum SDK, more specifically the cuStateVec library. This library is designed to optimize state-vector simulations on GPUs, which the authors demonstrated through a high bandwidth memory (HBM) and the parallelized architecture of CUDA cores. Here, they can simulate quantum circuits that were significantly faster than the traditional CPU-based simulators such as Qiskit's Aer. They compared this State-Vector approach of classical simulation with the Tensor Network approach (cuTensorNet), which would require representing the circuit as a graph of tensors that are contracted to find a result.

Another focus of their work was the application of GPU acceleration, specifically with the Variational Quantum Eigensolver (VQE). The VQE loop requires a repeated execution of a circuit ansatz with varying parameters, therefore presenting a Single Instruction, Multiple Data (SIMD) workload, which is a model under which GPUs excel. Bayraktar et al. focused primarily on a direct-attach model, where a GPU and the simulation logic resides on a single machine. Here, they compared the performance of the two methods of classical-quantum simulation (State-Vector and Tensor Network) and found for a VQE circuit, which normally contain high qubit counts (width) and low number of gate layers (depth), the state-vector method performs significantly better on a GPU due to the overhead of finding an optimal contraction path within a tensor network will often outweigh the raw memory throughput and the gate parallelism of GPUs for shallow circuits. 

In order to achieve the "quantum advantage" in a practical setting, the single node GPU model will need to be extended to distributed HPC infrastructures. This thesis builds upon the GPU-centric findings within Bayraktar et al.'s work by integrating MPI to manage the multi-node scaling, as while CUDA is addressing the internal parallelization of a single optimization step, MPI allows for the external parallelization of the entire workflow. By utilizing a multi-node cluster, this projects proposed software stack will be able to parallelize the gradient estimation of the VQE algorithm objective function. In this version, instead of evaluating circuit parameters serially, the MPI- based dispatcher can distribute different parameter sets across the multiple nodes in parallel. Each node will utilize CUDA kernels to pre-process data or to run local simulations before the final handshake with a cloud-based QPU. The "Triple-Heterogeneous" approach, which will utilize CPUs for orchestration, GPUs for numerical acceleration, and QPUs for quantum kernels, now directly handles the scalability limitations that exist in the single-host middleware models established earlier. 


\subsection{Implications for this Thesis}
This section's analysis of current quantum-classical integration research provides insights into the fundamental disconnect between high-level algorithm design and low-level hardware execution. The following points outline how this thesis will address the current identified gaps: 

\subsubsection{Addressing the Communication-Computation Disparity}
A main limitation was identified through the work of Weder et al. \cite{weder2021quantum}: the poor scaling that occurs in the handshake between the classical optimizers and the cloud-based QPUs. In the work of Peruzzo et al. \cite{peruzzo2014variational}, they avoided this bottleneck by utilizing local hardware integration (having access to an onsite QPU). While the local integration proved effective for their goals, a modern day hybrid stack must assume a cloud-based environment where the wall-clock time faces the issues of network latency ($\approx 100$--$500$ms) and queue times ($\approx 10$--$100$ms). This thesis will address this through an Asynchronous Dispatcher that uses a "latency hiding" strategy where the quantum speedup will overcompensate for the network latencies. The decoupling of the OpenQASM payload submissions from the classical optimization thread leaves the HPC nodes free to continue classical preprocessing, such as computing the next iteration's parameter updates. This ensures the CPU/GPU resources are not idle during the QPU's Round-Trip Time (RTT). 

Through the usage of the Hardware Efficient (HWE) ansatz and the Simultaneous  Perturbation Stochastic Approximation (SPSA) optimizer, latency would be further reduced. SPSA only requires two measurements per iteration in order to estimate the gradient of the expectation value, regardless of the number of parameters (as opposed to other optimizers that measure each parameter at a time) \cite{pellow2021classicoptimizers}. HWE utilizes shallow circuit depth, allowing the QPU to return results quickly (microseconds) \cite{mccaskey2019quantumchemistrybenchmarknearterm}.  Combined, these would require extra consideration within the stack, which will be explored later in this paper. 

\subsubsection{Scaling Beyond the Memory Wall}
As Bayrakter et al. \cite{bayraktar2023cuquantum} established, the state-vector simulation is one of the most efficient classical paths for a VQE-type circuit, however, it is limited by the VRAM of a single accelerator. This creates a "memory wall" where the simulation of molecules larger than $LiH$ (4 electrons, 631 Pauli terms, requiring 12 qubits) is unobtainable on a single workstation. This thesis adopts a distributed memory model using MPI to orchestrate the GPU-equipped nodes. Distributing the Pauli string expectation value estimations across a cluster allows the stack to aggregate the VRAM of the entire cluster to allow for larger chemical systems to be simulated and/or optimized (such as $BeH_2$ (6 electrons, 666 Pauli terms, requiring 14 qubits)) without exceeding the capacity of a single NVIDIA A100. 

\subsubsection{Optimization for SIMD Throughput}
Current examples of middleware frameworks, such as XACC \cite{mccaskey2018xacc} mainly focus on hardware portability (the portability of code) while overlooking the parallelism of parameters (the portability of workloads). VQE is a SIMD problem, where the same circuit is executed with thousands of parameter variations. By considering the SIMD-centric findings of Bayraktar et al. \cite{bayraktar2023cuquantum}, this stack incorporates Parameter Batching on the dispatcher level. Instead of a serial loop of submitting, waiting, and receiving, the middleware bundles variational points into a single job payload. This reduces the overhead from the quantum control stack and exploits the GPU ability to handle the high-throughput numerical gradients in parallel.
