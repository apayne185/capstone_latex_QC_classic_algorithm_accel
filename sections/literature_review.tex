\section{Literature Review}
This section is meant to explore the evolution of hybrid quantum-classical computing, focusing within the subjects of quantum
hardware limitations in the NISQ era, the development of variational algorithms, and the architecture requirements
for developing the orchestration of HPC within this proposed stack. 

\subsection{Fundamentals of Quantum Hardware} 
Quantum computing has evolved considerably in recent decades, offering potential speedups of polynomial and super-polynomial scales over sets of computationally expensive 
classical algorithms, although the speedups offered are often problem specific and can only offer advancements in select situations \parencite{auyeung2023nphard}. 
However, during our current NISQ era, the most effective method of utilizing quantum computers is by having it interfaced alongside a classical computing system, 
preferably in a HPC environment \parencite{rallis2025interfacingquantumcomputingsystems}. 
\textcite{auyeung2023nphard} proposed the outlook on quantum computing to be seen as more of a numerical toolbox that will
provide different processing units for different parts of computation, focusing on the combined potential of
processing units over the potential of quantum hardware alone. 

Fundamentally, quantum hardware differs considerably from their classical counterparts. 
The basic unit of quantum hardware, a qubit, exists in the superposition of basis states $|0\rangle$ and $|1\rangle$ (as opposed to a classical bit, which has the state of $0$ and $1$). 
During computation, qubits exploit the entanglement, superposition, and quantum measurement properties of quantum mechanics. 
However, the introduced benefits within qubits are counterbalanced by their major physical limitations, such as decoherence, scalability,
and quantum error correction, setting both a size and a time limit on computation. Quantum Error Mitigation (QEM)
is a NISQ method, accepting the hardware imperfections and choosing to work around limitations so as to continue seeing progress, including  
post-processing noisy data outputs from circuit runs \parencite{cai2023quantumerrormitigation}. Quantum Error Correction (QEC) is another major challenge, as the fragility of qubits
leads to complex errors, making reliability difficult to guarantee in quantum results \parencite{oukaira2025quantum}. 
Short-depth quantum circuits with observable estimation allows bypassing issues such as short coherency and quantum noise \parencite{peruzzo2014variational}, promoting variational methods.  

\subsection{The Emergence of Variational Quantum Algorithms}
The foundational framework that is explored within this middleware stack is the Variational Quantum Eigensolver (VQE), which was first introduced 
by \textcite{peruzzo2014variational}. Within their paper, the researchers used a photonic quantum 
chip to demonstrate its ability to calculate the ground state energy (the lowest possible energy state, considered its most stable configuration) of the molecule $He-H^{+}$  
(2 electrons, 6 Pauli terms, requiring 2 qubits) through a quantum circuit and a classical 
optimization loop run in a physically tight setup. Their findings established a functioning example of a hybrid system where
the QPU role is developed from a standalone component to instead be a coprocessor. However, it should be noted that 
the molecule in Peruzzo's research was a minimal possible VQE experiment, chosen to be a proof-of-concept
on the hardware in 2014. This proposed stack extends their findings in  
VQE research to handle more complex molecules while also operating on a cloud-based hybrid system. 

VQEs are one of two major Variational Quantum Algorithms (VQAs) 
used in solving optimization problems that have been proven difficult to solve due to incomplete or
uncertain data. VQAs require an ansatz, a parameterized quantum circuit whose form determines what variational parameters are used and how to specifically train
them within a costly tuning process to minimize a cost function~\parencite{auyeung2023nphard}. \textcite{mccaskey2019quantumchemistrybenchmarknearterm}
discussed the current options for ansatzes in quantum systems, such as the Unitary Coupled Cluster (UCC) and the Hardware Effective (HWE) ansatzes.  
Within VQE, they explore how UCC ansatzes provide an indepth, electron number-conserving procedure to describe trial wavefunctions but will yield a deep circuit
for even smaller systems, while HWE ansatzes will offer a shallower circuit depth and can support a larger parameter space. However, it is firmly established that
HWE prepares quantum states with a varying number of electrons that often yield unphysical and inaccurate results for their tasks. For chemistry applications, 
UCC was the logical choice as it is designed to mimic the physical interactions of electrons within molecules, 
however, the depth required by the circuit created by this ansatz is deep and impractical to implement due to coherency 
limitations. A truncated version of UCC, UCCSD, is cut at doubles while still capturing the vast majority of electron 
correlation energy~\parencite{romero2018strategiesquantumcomputingmolecular}, providing a moderately shallower circuit depth.  
Whereas HWE is the hardware-considerate approach, with shallow circuits that execute with fewer complications on noisy 
hardware and utilizes gates that are specific for the hardware optimal performance. However, research has proven convergence 
to be more difficult for HWE as it struggles with barren plateaus due to its random starting parameters (random, abstract mathematical 
rotations instead of electron movements)\parencite{kandala2017hardware}.  

Peruzzo et al. established through the usage of the Variational Principle, stating that the ground energy state $E_0$ 
will always be lesser than or equal to the expectation value of $H$ (the time-independent Hamiltonian that is calculated 
with the trial Hamiltonian wavefunction $|\psi(\theta)\rangle$). 
\begin{equation}
     E_0 \leq \langle \psi(\theta) | \hat{H} | \psi(\theta) \rangle
    \label{eq:variational_principle}
\end{equation}
In quantum chemistry, methods such as Hartree-Fock (HF) approximation and Full Configuration Interaction (FCI) determine the motion of electrons within the fields of other electrons. HF provides a 
readable equation of individual electrons for a best approximate of a wave function's ground state. FCI, on the other hand, is considered a benchmark method as it 
fully expands the wave function to describe every potential electron interaction, resulting in a  
heavy computational cost that renders FCI intractable for anything but the smallest molecules \parencite{szabo1996modernquantumchemistry}. 
VQE implementations are initialized near the HF approximation reference state, using the parameterized ansatz for an iterative improvement of its
estimates to approach the exact FCI energy. Here, the variation principle is used to prove $E_{HF} \geq E_{VQE} \geq E_{FCI}$ 
will hold for any ansatzes that conserve particle numbers \parencite{peruzzo2014variational,szabo1996modernquantumchemistry}.  

Within their experiments, \textcite{peruzzo2014variational} compensated for NISQ coherency limitations by offloading the objective function
evaluation to the QPU using a classical Nelder-Mead (NM) simplex optimizer.
In a comparison study done by \textcite{pellow2021classicoptimizers} 
between sets of gradient-based and gradient-free classical optimizers for a Variational Quantum Linear Solver, a different gradient-free optimizer 
Simultaneous Perturbation Stochastic Approximation (SPSA) outperformed all other contestants, including NM, in state vector simulation. 
Specifically within realistic noise models with depolarizing and hardware noise, SPSA outperformed NM and other gradient-free alternatives, making them a desirable, robust optimizer when interacting with physical, noisy qubits. 

While the results of \textcite{peruzzo2014variational} did prove the viability of the hybrid approach, it was conducted on a small-scale, 
integrated system where the communication overhead is negligible, with a local photonic chip directly interfaced with a classical controller 
for predictable low-latency in sending variational parameters to the QPU and receiving measured expectation values. 
This physically tight setup is an unrealistic model for modern research experiment environments where hardware resources would be physically distant, avoiding the reality of 
modern day QC serialization overhead, network latencies, and Round-Trip-Time (RTT) penalties. 
If a hybrid stack is not optimized, the potential wait for data transmission could exceed the time spent on actual computation within adaptive algorithms like VQE, as identified by \textcite{cruise2020practicalquantumcomputingvalue}
demonstrated within cloud native CPU-QPU environments, the RTT latency exceeds the circuit execution time by a factor of 40, which degrades the algorithmic performance. They showed 
that sustaining a consitent circuit stream to their 150-qubit system would require minimum 1.2,GB/s bandwidth. This argues for a middleware layer that is
capable of efficient resource dispatching,  as without it the "communication-to-computation" ratio will scale poorly and deny the potential 
speedup offered within these hybrid systems. This proposed stack addresses the identified architectural inefficiencies 
through prioritizing low-latency dispatching and parallelized classical preprocessing. 

\textcite{kandala2017hardware} explored the specific feasibility of using HWE ansatz with a SPSA optimizer 
in a VQE setup to determine the ground state energy for the molecules of $H_2, LiH, BeH_{2}$ (with $2,4,6$ qubits, depth=1), increasing in complexity, Hamiltonian size, and number of Pauli terms. 
They contrast their setup with a UCCSD ansatz, where the gates of molecules such as $BeH_{2}$ do not finish before the window of qubit coherency closed. 
They adapted the HWE to only use single qubit rotations and entangling gates that matched the chip's coupling map, resulting in quick execution of shallow circuits but with the cost of degraded particle number conservation.  
Their findings are further extended in this stack by the use of distributed HPC resources for faster computation. 

\subsection{Classical-Quantum Middleware and Orchestration}
High Performance Computing (HPC) systems extend the abilities of classical computation through clusters of CPUs, GPUs, and 
other specialized accelerators, enabling for parallel computation at scale \parencite{rallis2025interfacingquantumcomputingsystems}. 

The current industry standard for a hardware independent quantum integration process was developed by \textcite{mccaskey2018xacc} 
within their eXtreme ACCelerator (XACC) hybrid framework. They acknowledge the current vendors, including Rigetti,
IBM, Google, etc., all provide specific quantum programming languages for each version of quantum hardware, without 
a method of integrating quantum software with hardware or transcending the variety of computational specifications. This leaves users to manually develop the 
often complex communication between the hybrid components, including the development of the compiler workflow steps of instruction scheduling, routing, and error mitigation. 
To address this issue, XACC consists of a system-level infrastructure with low-level software to manage the QPU, treating it as a specialized 
coprocessor (similar to the approach of \textcite{peruzzo2014variational} and \textcite{rallis2025interfacingquantumcomputingsystems}) which allows researchers to offload 
quantum kernels from the classical host to various backend accelerators by using a unified API. By abstracting the hardware and focusing on
development of an intuitive, user-friendly Pythonic interface for interactions with the QPU, McCaskey et al. set the current standard for quantum-classical middleware.  

While the XACC framework did successfully achieve their goal of language and hardware portability, McCaskey et al.'s implementation 
developed compiler level integration that translates between QPUs and quantum programming languages within an execution model of a singular CPU and QPU. 
They concentrated solely on communication between the classical and quantum computers (the architectural connection) without addressing the bottlenecks of a high scale of 
classical optimization subroutines, which could be managed through distributed resources. The XACC model is synchronous, with the  
classical host pushing a circuit to the QPU then waiting for the measurement outcome, which is adequate for a single process model 
but is limiting if the system grows to require distributed parallelism. This proposed stack builds 
upon XACC and its modular, language/hardware agnostic philosophy to emphasize orchestration priority for system scaling and parallelization by 
integrating Message Passing Interface (MPI) and CUDA for asynchronous execution, as otherwise classical resources remain idle while a single QPU processes a circuit. 
It is designed with the intention of future multi-node deployment but is validated on a single host, done to bypass single process performance limitations. 

\subsection{HPC Integration and Parallelization} 
Early research on hybrid quantum-classical systems largely focused on exploring the feasibility of executing quantum kernels (quantum code)
within isolated, often experimental setups that do not reflect the reality of modern quantum interactions. 
However, \textcite{bayraktar2023cuquantum} proposed a method to handle the computational intensity of classical overhead 
within hybrid systems utilizing the NVIDIA cuQuantum SDK, an industry standard library of primitives for GPU accelerated quantum circuit simulations. 
They identified the pre-cuQuantum bottleneck to be the classical simulation and expectation value estimation, as the statevector scales exponentially ($2^n$) with the number 
of required qubits ($n$) and the corresponding complexity of the variational ansatz. Classical statevector simulation computes 
the full vector, providing a noiseless, exact result of expectation values with a significant memory cost that doubles with every qubit. This is a bottleneck that a serial 
CPU architecture alone cannot manage, as once the size of the state vector exceeds the processing capacity of a single CPU 
it requires distribution across multiple nodes in order to reduce the exponential memory cost.  
Bayraktar et al.'s research evaluates the NVIDIA cuQuantum SDK on A100 GPUs, more specifically the 
cuStateVec library that is designed to optimize statevector simulations on GPUs, demonstrated through high bandwidth memory (HBM) 
and parallelized architecture of CUDA cores, where they simulated quantum circuits that were significantly faster than 
the traditional CPU-based simulators (such as Qiskit Aer). Their findings for state vector simulation on the 
A100 GPU proved that gate applications are memory-bandwidth bound (instead of compute bound), 
as the A100 sustained 1.6-1.7 TB/s bandwidth on their tested operations, with the gate application time scaling linearly
with the number of gates, the main limitation being how long it takes to read/write the $2^n$ statevector from A100's HBM2e memory \parencite{bayraktar2023cuquantum}. 

Another focus of their work was a GPU acceleration QAOA circuit, where comparisons can be drawn between 
QAOA and a VQE implementation as they share circuit characteristics, such as high qubit counts (circuit width) and lower gate depths (a SIMD workload suited for GPUs). 
Bayraktar et al. focused their research objectives on a model where a GPU and the simulation
logic reside on a single machine to compare the performances of 
State Vector (SV) and Tensor Network (TN) simulation. In their QAOA findings, the SV method performs significantly better on a GPU, as results 
between a single DGX A100 GPU and dual CPUs showed a  5.9-30x speedup. However, scaling to 8 A100s in a single DGX node, 
there was a 70-290x speedup over CPUs, and with multi-GPU strong scaling (in one node) there was 4.6-7.0x from 1-8 GPUs.
On GPUs, state vectors are significantly faster with results that can be 
improved through the use of multi-node GPUs to distribute terms, as their findings for multi-GPU scaling proved its limitation 
results from interconnect bandwidth. \parencite{bayraktar2023cuquantum}

In order to achieve the "quantum advantage" \parencite{harrow2017quantumsupremacy} in a practical setting, the single node GPU model will need to be extended to 
distributed HPC infrastructures. This thesis builds upon the GPU-centric findings within Bayraktar et al.'s work by integrating MPI to 
manage the distributed scaling, as while CUDA addresses the internal parallelization of a single optimization step, MPI 
allows for the external parallelization of the entire workflow across multiple processes. This proposed project is designed for a multi-node environment but is validated on a single host with multiple MPI processes, 
where the software stack is able to parallelize the gradient estimation of the VQE algorithm objective function. In this version, 
instead of evaluating circuit parameters serially, the MPI-based dispatcher can distribute different parameter sets across the multiple 
ranks in parallel. Each rank will utilize CUDA kernels for statevector construction and evaluating Pauli terms before the final handshake with a 
cloud-based QPU. This "Triple-Heterogeneous" approach uses CPUs for orchestration, GPUs for numerical acceleration, and QPUs 
for quantum kernels, directly handling scalability limitations that existed in the single-process middleware models established earlier. 

The gaps identified above of the CPU-QPU communication bottleneck \parencite{cruise2020practicalquantumcomputingvalue}, the statevector bandwidth bottleneck, and 
the current lack of distributed parallelization in existing middleware all directly influence the developed architecture in the following section.
